Every year there's a new design trend. Glassmorphism. Bento grids. Claymorphism. Brutalism. Dark mode everything. Gradients are back. No wait, flat is back.

And every year, studios build their portfolio sites around these trends, clients request them, and developers implement them — only for the whole thing to look dated eighteen months later.

I want to make a case for the boring alternative.

## What boring design actually means

Boring design isn't bad design. It's design that prioritises function over novelty, clarity over cleverness, and longevity over trend-chasing.

It means:

- Typography that's easy to read, not typography that makes a statement
- Spacing that gives content room to breathe, not spacing that looks "editorial"
- Colour used to direct attention, not colour used to signal personality
- Interactions that help users accomplish tasks, not interactions designed to impress

It means making decisions that you'd still be happy with in three years.

## Why trends are expensive

When you build around a trend, you're taking on debt. Not technical debt — aesthetic debt.

The more your design relies on a specific visual moment — a particular shade of purple, a specific type of blur, a certain kind of illustration style — the more it will need to be rebuilt when that moment passes.

> Timeless design is not about being boring. It's about being deliberate.

The brands and products that have aged best — Stripe, Apple, The Guardian — aren't the ones that chased trends. They're the ones that established a clear visual language and maintained it consistently over time.

## The components of durable design

### A restrained colour palette

Pick two or three colours and use them consistently. One primary, one neutral, one accent. That's it. Resist the urge to add more.

### Typography as the primary tool

Most of the work in good design is done by type. Hierarchy, rhythm, emphasis — all of this can be achieved through typography alone. A strong typeface, used well, is more powerful than any visual effect.

### Whitespace as a feature

Amateur design fills space. Professional design uses it. Generous whitespace isn't laziness — it's confidence. It says: the content is strong enough to stand on its own.

### Systematic thinking

Instead of designing individual screens, design systems. A button component, a card component, a spacing scale. When you think in systems, you make decisions once and apply them consistently — which automatically produces cohesion.

---

## The practical argument

Beyond aesthetics, boring design is cheaper to maintain.

When you don't use a trendy technique, you don't have to rebuild it when the trend fades. When you use standard components, onboarding new developers is easier. When you document your system, handoffs go smoothly.

Boring design ships faster, costs less to maintain, and ages better.

---

The next time you're tempted to add a glowing neon effect or an elaborate scroll animation — ask yourself: will this still feel right in 2027?

If the answer is uncertain, you probably don't need it.
