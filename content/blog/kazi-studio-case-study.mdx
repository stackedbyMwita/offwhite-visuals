Kazi Studio is a Nairobi-based architecture and interior design firm founded by a husband-and-wife team with over fifteen years of combined experience. When they came to us, they had no brand identity to speak of — just a name, a portfolio of excellent work, and a growing reputation that deserved a stronger visual presence.

This is the story of how we built their brand from nothing in four weeks.

## The brief

The brief, as stated: *"We want to look like we belong in the same conversation as the best architecture firms in the world — without losing the fact that we're proudly Kenyan."*

That's an excellent brief. It's specific, it has tension, and it has aspiration. The best briefs do all three.

The tension — world-class but distinctly local — would become the creative engine for the entire project.

## Discovery

Our discovery session with the Kazi Studio founders lasted three hours. A few things emerged that shaped everything that followed:

**On their work**: They described their approach as "restraint." They believed that good architecture removes things — that the best spaces are defined by what's not there. This was immediately interesting as a creative constraint.

**On their clients**: Primarily high-net-worth residential clients and corporate tenants. People with taste, who would spot the difference between something generic and something considered.

**On the name**: "Kazi" is Swahili for "work." They chose it deliberately — to signify craft, effort, and the dignity of doing something well. This gave us a cultural anchor.

## The visual directions

We presented three directions after one week of development.

### Direction 1 — "The Grid"
Inspired by architectural drawing conventions. Monochromatic. Structured. The logo used a custom letterform where the K was constructed from perpendicular lines, referencing floor plans and technical drawings.

### Direction 2 — "The Mark"
A single, strong geometric symbol — an abstraction of a threshold, a doorway. Minimal. Premium. Intended to work as a standalone mark without the wordmark.

### Direction 3 — "The Weight"
Heavy typography as the primary visual tool. A custom-drawn wordmark in a modified condensed serif, with significant contrast between thick and thin strokes — referencing the contrast between solid and void in architecture.

The clients chose Direction 3. They connected immediately with the typography-led approach, and the reference to architectural contrast was exactly right for the "restraint" positioning.

## Refinement

Over the following ten days, we refined the chosen direction through two formal revision rounds.

Key decisions made during refinement:

**The colour system**: Off-white and deep charcoal as the primary palette — deliberate neutrality. A single warm gold as the only accent colour, used very sparingly. The off-white was not pure white — it had a slight warmth that referenced natural materials: stone, concrete, light wood.

**The wordmark**: We redrew the letterforms by hand, balancing the weight contrast more precisely. The final K has a stroke ratio of approximately 7:1 — dramatic but not decorative.

**The spacing**: The tracking on the wordmark was set significantly wider than standard, referencing the breathing space in well-designed interiors.

## System development

From the wordmark, we built out the full system:

- **Logo suite**: Primary horizontal, stacked, icon-only monogram (KS), reversed on dark
- **Typography**: The wordmark typeface (licensed) for display, a refined grotesque for body text
- **Colour**: Off-white, charcoal, warm gold, mid-grey — each with specific usage rules
- **Photography guidelines**: Natural light. Real materials. No renders. No stock.
- **Stationery system**: Business cards, letterhead, envelopes — all designed
- **Digital presence guidelines**: Social media templates, email signature

## The outcome

The guidelines document was 48 pages.

Within three months of launching the new brand, Kazi Studio reported three new high-value client inquiries that specifically mentioned the professionalism of their materials as a factor in making contact.

> "For the first time, our visual identity actually represents the quality of our work. Clients come in already impressed — before we've shown them a single project."

That's the measure of a successful brand identity.

The work that mattered most on this project wasn't the logo. It was the hour spent in discovery understanding what "restraint" meant to them — and then finding a way to make that word visible.
