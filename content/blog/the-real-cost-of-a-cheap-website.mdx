A $300 website sounds like a bargain. And in the short term, it is — you have something to point people to, a URL on your business card, a checkbox ticked.

But let's do the actual math.

## What you get for $300

At this price point, you're typically getting:

- A theme-based website built on WordPress or Wix
- Generic stock photography
- Template copy, lightly edited
- No custom design work
- No performance optimisation
- No SEO foundation
- Minimal support after delivery

This isn't a criticism of the people building at this price — it's simply what's possible at that budget. Time is the primary input to quality work, and $300 buys very little of it.

## The hidden costs

### Cost 1 — Lost trust

Your website is often the first interaction a potential client has with your business. First impressions are formed in milliseconds and are extremely difficult to reverse.

A generic-looking website communicates, however unfairly, that the business behind it may also be generic. For service businesses especially — where the client is buying expertise and trust — this is an expensive signal to send.

### Cost 2 — Missed conversions

A well-designed website with clear messaging and a single call to action converts visitors into leads. A poorly designed one doesn't.

Assume your site gets 500 visitors a month. If a well-designed site converts at 3% and a poorly designed one converts at 0.5%, the difference is 12.5 leads per month. At an average client value of $1,000 — that's $12,500 per month in missed revenue.

Over a year, that's $150,000.

Your $300 website is not saving you money. It's costing you far more than that.

### Cost 3 — The rebuild

Low-cost websites are almost never built to last. The theme becomes incompatible with the latest version of the platform. The plugins conflict. The developer is unreachable. The site slows down.

Within two to three years, most clients with cheap websites are rebuilding — paying again, sometimes more than if they'd invested properly the first time.

### Cost 4 — Your time

Cheap websites require more of your time. More back-and-forth trying to explain what you want to someone who doesn't have the expertise to realise it. More troubleshooting when things break. More workarounds when the platform doesn't support what you need.

Your time has a value. Factor it in.

---

## What good websites actually cost

A professionally designed and built website — custom, fast, mobile-optimised, with strong copy and a clear conversion strategy — typically costs between $2,000 and $10,000 depending on scope.

This is not a luxury. It's infrastructure.

For a business generating $100,000+ per year, spending $5,000 on a website that converts better, builds more trust, and lasts five years is not an expense. It's one of the highest-ROI investments you can make.

---

The question isn't whether you can afford a good website. It's whether you can afford not to have one.
