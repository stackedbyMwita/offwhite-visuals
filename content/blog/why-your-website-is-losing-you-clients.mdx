Most business websites don't fail because they look bad. They fail because they make visitors work too hard.

Within the first few seconds of landing on your site, a visitor is asking three questions:

- **What is this?**
- **Is this for me?**
- **What should I do next?**

If your website can't answer all three — clearly and immediately — you're losing them.

![A cluttered homepage with five competing CTAs — a common conversion killer](/assets/blog/cluttered-homepage.jfif)

## The most common ways websites fail

### 1. The hero section explains nothing

"Empowering businesses to reach their full potential" tells me nothing about what you do. Your hero section should state, in plain language, exactly what you offer and who it's for.

Compare:

> *"Empowering businesses to reach their full potential."*

To:

> *"We build fast, beautiful websites for Nairobi-based businesses who want to stand out online."*

The second version answers all three questions in one sentence.

### 2. No clear next step

Every page should have one obvious thing it wants the visitor to do. Not three. Not five. One.

If your homepage has a "Contact Us" button, a "Learn More" link, a newsletter signup, and a "Book a Call" form all competing for attention — you've given visitors decision paralysis. Most will leave without doing any of them.

### 3. The site is slow

A one-second delay in page load time can reduce conversions by up to 7%. On mobile, the tolerance is even lower.

Common culprits:
- Unoptimised images (the number one offender)
- Too many third-party scripts loading on every page
- No caching strategy
- Hosting on a shared server in the wrong geography

### 4. It doesn't work on mobile

Over 60% of web traffic is now mobile. If your site was designed desktop-first and "made to work" on mobile as an afterthought, visitors are noticing.

Test your site on an actual phone. Not just in Chrome's device emulator — an actual phone. You'll spot problems immediately.

### 5. The copy talks about you, not the visitor

"We were founded in 2015 with a passion for excellence" — your visitor does not care. Not yet.

They care about themselves. They care about their problem. Write your copy from their perspective first.

## How to fix it

Start with an audit. Go through your site as if you've never seen it before — or better, ask someone who hasn't to use it while you watch.

Then address issues in this order:

1. **Clarity of message** — Can a stranger tell what you do in under 5 seconds?
2. **Single call to action** — Remove competing CTAs and focus each page on one outcome
3. **Performance** — Run your site through PageSpeed Insights and fix the top issues
4. **Mobile experience** — Test on real devices
5. **Copy** — Rewrite your headlines to focus on the visitor's problem, not your credentials

You don't need a complete redesign. Most sites need a rewrite of three headlines and one clearer call to action — and conversions improve immediately.

---

A website that converts isn't necessarily the most beautiful one. It's the clearest one.
