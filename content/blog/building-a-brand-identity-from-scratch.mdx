Brand identity projects are some of the most nuanced work we do. Unlike a website — where success is measurable and feedback is relatively concrete — a brand identity requires a client to trust a visual direction they haven't seen yet, based on a brief they may not have been sure how to write.

This is a walkthrough of our process, from the first conversation to the final deliverable.

## Phase 1 — Discovery

Before opening Figma, we spend time understanding the business.

The discovery session is a two-hour conversation — sometimes more — covering:

- **Positioning**: Who are your customers? What do they believe before they find you? What do you want them to believe after?
- **Competitors**: Who else is in this space? What do they look like? What do they communicate?
- **Personality**: If your brand were a person, how would they dress? How would they speak? What would they never say?
- **Aspiration**: What brands do you admire, even outside your industry? Why?

The output of discovery isn't a document — it's alignment. By the end, we should both be able to describe the brand in the same words.

## Phase 2 — Concept development

We develop three distinct visual directions — not variations on a theme, but genuinely different interpretations of the brief.

Each concept includes:
- A moodboard
- A primary logotype
- A colour palette
- A typography system
- One application mockup

The point of showing three directions is not to give the client options to cherry-pick from. It's to make explicit the choices that were implicit in the brief. Clients often don't know what they want until they see what they don't want.

> The purpose of presenting options is to start a conversation, not to end one.

## Phase 3 — Refinement

One direction is chosen — or sometimes two are merged — and we refine it.

Refinement is where the real work happens. This is where letterforms get adjusted by single pixels, where colour ratios are balanced for accessibility, where the logo is tested at 16px and at 300px.

We typically go through two formal revision rounds in this phase, plus informal check-ins throughout.

## Phase 4 — System development

A logo is not a brand identity. A brand identity is a system.

In this phase, we extend the visual language into a complete system:

- **Logo suite**: primary, horizontal, stacked, icon-only, reversed
- **Colour system**: primary, secondary, neutral, semantic
- **Typography scale**: display, heading, body, caption, mono
- **Spacing and layout principles**
- **Photography and illustration guidelines**
- **Voice and tone guidelines**

## Phase 5 — Documentation and handoff

The brand guidelines document is the final deliverable — and it's as important as the visual work itself.

A brand identity that isn't documented will drift the moment it leaves our hands. The guidelines exist to preserve the intent of the system for everyone who uses it after us — in-house designers, developers, marketing teams, printers.

We deliver:
- Brand guidelines PDF (typically 30–60 pages)
- Logo files in SVG, PNG, PDF, and EPS formats
- Colour values in HEX, RGB, CMYK, and Pantone
- Font files or web font specifications
- A shared Figma library with all components

---

The measure of a successful brand identity project isn't how good the logo looks on the day of delivery. It's how well the system holds up twelve months later, when ten different people have applied it in ten different contexts.

That's what we're designing for.
